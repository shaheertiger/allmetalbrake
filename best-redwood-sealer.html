Why Redwood Needs Sealing Despite Its Natural Resistance

Redwood has a well-earned reputation as one of the most naturally durable lumber species in North America. Its heartwood contains tannins and natural oils that resist rot, fungal decay, and insect damage far better than pressure-treated pine, cedar, or most other softwoods. A redwood fence post set in direct ground contact will outlast an untreated pine post by decades. This natural durability is why redwood has been prized for outdoor construction since the 19th century. But "naturally resistant" is not the same as "maintenance-free." Left unprotected and unsealed, even the best redwood will begin to show visible deterioration within a single season — and the damage accelerates with each passing year.

The single biggest threat to unsealed redwood is ultraviolet radiation from sunlight. UV rays break down lignin — the polymer that holds wood fibers together and gives redwood its characteristic warm reddish-brown color. As lignin degrades, the surface fibers become gray, loose, and rough, a process called photodegradation. Within 6 to 12 months of initial exposure, a beautiful piece of fresh redwood lumber can turn a flat silver-gray. The color change is purely cosmetic at first, but the structural breakdown underneath becomes a real problem over time: a gray, weathered surface is weaker, more absorbent, and far more susceptible to the moisture cycles that eventually cause checking, splitting, and decay.

Moisture is the second major threat. Although redwood's tannins do provide genuine rot resistance deep in the heartwood, the surface of any outdoor wood is subject to constant wetting and drying cycles — morning dew, rain, irrigation overspray — that cause the wood fibers to swell and contract. This repeated movement is what causes surface checking: the small cracks that appear along the grain of boards exposed to the weather. Checking itself is not structurally serious, but the cracks trap dirt, harbor mildew, and allow moisture to penetrate deeper into the wood, eventually reaching zones where rot can take hold, especially at joints, notches, and end grain.

Tannin bleed is a phenomenon specific to redwood and western red cedar that many homeowners don't anticipate. When redwood gets wet, its water-soluble tannins can leach out of the wood and stain adjacent surfaces — concrete, brick, painted siding, or stone pavers. On a deck, this shows up as reddish-brown streaks on the concrete below. A quality sealer locks the tannins in the wood and dramatically reduces bleed. For new redwood adjacent to any painted or masonry surface, this is not a minor concern: it's one of the primary practical reasons to seal as soon as possible after installation.

⚠️ Key Takeaway Redwood's natural rot resistance protects the structural core, but it does NOT protect the surface from UV graying, moisture checking, or tannin bleed. Sealing is essential within 30–60 days of installation for any redwood exposed to the elements.

Mildew and algae are also concerns in wet climates. Redwood's tannins do suppress fungal growth to some degree, but in shaded, damp environments — the north side of a house, under tree canopy, in humid coastal regions — green algae and black mildew will colonize an unsealed surface just as readily as on any other wood. Most quality redwood sealers include mildewcide additives that provide ongoing protection. Finally, dirt and grime accumulation on an unsealed surface is harder to clean and can permanently stain wood fibers if allowed to work into the grain over multiple seasons.

Clear vs. Tinted Sealer on Redwood

Choosing between a clear sealer, a semi-transparent stain/sealer, and a solid color stain is one of the most important decisions you'll make for a redwood project. Each option has genuine tradeoffs in terms of appearance, protection, longevity, and maintenance. The comparison below reflects two seasons of real-world testing on matched redwood sample boards.

For most redwood projects — especially new decks, fences, and furniture — a semi-transparent oil-based penetrating stain/sealer is the optimal choice. It provides the best balance of UV protection and color enhancement without hiding the grain that makes redwood worth the investment in the first place. Semi-transparent products also offer the most forgiving maintenance profile: when the finish begins to fade (rather than peel), you simply clean the surface and reapply without stripping. Clear sealers are a reasonable choice for fresh-cut redwood where you want to preserve the natural color precisely as it is, but they require more frequent reapplication and provide less UV protection. Solid stains are typically reserved for very old, gray, or weathered redwood where the natural appearance has already been lost and cosmetic coverage is the goal.

💡 Pro Tip: The Tinted Clear Option Several manufacturers offer "natural" or "cedar" tinted clears — products that add just enough warm pigment to enhance redwood's natural tone while still maintaining an almost-clear appearance. TWP 100 in "Redwood" and Ready Seal in "Natural Cedar" are excellent examples. These outperform true clears on UV protection while still showing nearly all of the natural grain.

How Redwood Differs From Cedar and PT Pine as a Sealing Substrate

Understanding what makes redwood unique as a wood species helps explain why not all sealers perform equally well on it — and why some products marketed for "cedar and redwood" actually work better on cedar.

Compared to western red cedar, redwood has a slightly coarser grain and larger pores. This means it can absorb more sealer per square foot, which is an advantage for penetrating oil formulas — they can soak deeper into the wood for better protection. But it also means that thin, water-based sealers that rely on surface film formation may not cover as uniformly, requiring more careful application to avoid lap marks. Cedar tends to absorb evenly and smoothly; redwood's variable grain can cause uneven uptake if the wood is applied when too dry or in direct sun.

Redwood's tannin content is distinctly higher than cedar's, and this matters for two reasons. First, those tannins can react chemically with certain sealer chemistries — particularly water-based formulas with high pH levels — causing a temporary darkening or greenish discoloration on application that fades as the sealer cures. Second, tannin bleed is a much larger concern with redwood than with cedar. Any sealer you choose for redwood should ideally be tested on a small hidden area first to confirm it doesn't cause unexpected color shift.

Pressure-treated (PT) pine is the most different of the three. PT lumber is saturated with preservative chemicals (modern ACQ or CA treatments) that must fully cure — typically 60–90 days — before any sealer or stain will adhere properly. PT pine also lacks the natural tannins and oils of redwood, making it more uniformly porous and generally easier to coat, but it requires more frequent reapplication because it has no natural chemical defense. Redwood's natural oils actually help bind oil-based sealers better over time, which is one reason oil-based penetrating formulas consistently outperform water-based ones on redwood even when water-based products work acceptably on PT pine.

How to Prepare Redwood for Sealing

Proper preparation is the single biggest factor determining how long your sealer will last. A premium product applied to poorly prepared wood will underperform a mid-range product applied correctly every time. Preparation approach differs significantly between new and weathered redwood.

New Redwood (less than 3 months old)

Fresh-cut or recently installed redwood contains a high level of natural moisture and mill glaze — a compressed surface layer created by the saw and planing equipment. Mill glaze seals the wood's pores and prevents proper sealer absorption. Before sealing new redwood, perform the soak test: sprinkle water on the surface. If the water beads up and doesn't absorb within 30 seconds, the wood is not ready to accept sealer. For mill-glazed lumber, light sanding with 80-grit paper or a light pressure wash (1,200–1,500 PSI maximum) will open the pores. Allow the wood to dry completely — typically 48–72 hours minimum in good drying conditions — before applying sealer.

For new redwood that's been rained on or wet, wait for it to fully dry to below 19% moisture content (use an inexpensive wood moisture meter). Applying oil-based sealer over wet wood traps moisture and prevents proper penetration. For new dry wood that passes the soak test, no cleaning or brightening is needed — a light rinse to remove sawdust and debris is sufficient.

Weathered Redwood (gray or partially gray)

Weathered redwood requires a two-step cleaning process. First, apply a wood cleaner/brightener — products like Defy Wood Cleaner or TWP Wash — to remove the gray dead wood fibers and surface contamination. These cleaners typically contain oxalic acid or a similar mild bleaching agent. Apply with a brush or pump sprayer, let dwell 10–15 minutes, then scrub and rinse thoroughly with a garden hose or low-pressure washer (under 1,800 PSI, use a fan tip). The wood should lighten noticeably — a gray board should return to something close to its original warm tone before you apply any sealer.

After cleaning, apply a wood brightener (most cleaner kits include this as a second step). Brighteners neutralize the alkaline cleaner residue and open the wood pores to maximize sealer absorption. Allow the wood to dry completely (typically 48–72 hours) before sealing. Do not apply sealer if rain is forecast within 48 hours.

⚠️ Pressure Washer Warning Never use a pressure washer above 1,500 PSI on redwood. High-pressure washing raises grain severely and can damage the soft earlywood between the grain lines. Use a fan-pattern tip and keep the wand moving. Damage from over-aggressive pressure washing is permanent and will show under any sealer.

6-Step Application Guide for Redwood Sealer

1Check Weather Conditions

Apply only when air and wood temperature is between 50–90°F, no rain forecast for 48 hours, and humidity below 70%. Avoid direct intense sunlight during application — work in shade or on a cool, overcast day to prevent lap marks from premature drying.

2Final Surface Preparation

Confirm wood is clean, dry (moisture content under 19%), and passes the soak test. Remove any hardware, furniture, or items adjacent to work area. Mask off any adjacent masonry or painted surfaces to protect from tannin drips.

3Mix/Stir the Product

Stir oil-based sealers thoroughly — do not shake. Pigments and UV absorbers settle. Box multiple cans (mix them together in a larger bucket) for large projects to ensure color consistency across the entire application.

4Apply the First Coat

Use a natural-bristle brush for oil-based products (synthetic for water-based). Work with the grain, applying liberally. On a deck, work board by board to avoid lap marks. Backbrush immediately to work the product deep into the grain and prevent puddles in low spots or knots.

5Apply the Second Coat (if needed)

For weathered or rough-sawn redwood, apply a second coat within the manufacturer's specified window (typically 1–4 hours for oil-based, while the first coat is still tacky). On fresh, smooth redwood, a single liberal coat is typically sufficient for penetrating formulas.

6Wipe Excess and Allow to Cure

After 45–60 minutes, check for any unabsorbed product pooling on the surface. Wipe off excess with a clean rag before it becomes tacky — pooled sealer will form sticky spots that attract dirt. Keep the surface dry and off foot traffic for the manufacturer's recommended cure time (typically 24–48 hours for oil-based products).

How to Restore Gray/Weathered Redwood Before Sealing

Gray redwood is not a lost cause. Unless the wood has deep structural checking, splitting, or active rot, even severely weathered gray redwood can be restored to a significant portion of its original appearance with the right approach. Here's the process our testing team used to restore 18-month-gray redwood samples to near-original appearance:

Step 1: Assess the damage. Probe the wood with a scratch awl or screwdriver. If the tool penetrates more than ¼" with light pressure, the wood has active rot and needs replacement rather than restoration. If it's just surface gray with solid wood beneath, proceed.

Step 2: Clean with a dedicated wood cleaner. Products like TWP Wash, Defy Wood Cleaner, or Cabot Problem Solver Wood Cleaner contain sodium hypochlorite (bleach) or oxalic acid to dissolve and flush out the gray dead fiber, mold, and mildew from the surface. Apply generously, allow to dwell 15 minutes (keep it wet — don't let it dry on the wood), scrub with a stiff-bristle brush, and rinse thoroughly.

Step 3: Apply a wood brightener. Brighteners typically contain oxalic acid, which chemically bleaches the tannin oxidation compounds responsible for the darkest gray tones and neutralizes the cleaner's alkalinity to bring the wood back to its natural pH. Apply with a brush or pump sprayer, scrub lightly, and rinse. The wood should lighten dramatically — from gray to something approaching tan or light red-brown.

Step 4: Allow full drying (48–72 hours minimum). Gray wood that has lost its natural oils is very thirsty and will absorb atmospheric humidity aggressively. A moisture meter reading below 19% before sealing is critical — more critical than for new wood, because weathered wood's open grain can trap moisture under a sealer if not fully dried.

Step 5: Apply a penetrating restoring oil. For best color restoration results, use Cabot Australian Timber Oil or Penofin. Apply liberally, allow to penetrate for 30 minutes, then apply a second coat. The oil will dramatically deepen and warm the color of the restored wood. Follow up with a second-coat application of TWP 100 or Armstrong Clark within the wet-on-wet window for best protection longevity.

California VOC Regulations Affecting Sealer Choices

California has the strictest VOC (volatile organic compound) regulations in the United States, implemented by regional air quality management districts. The two most significant are the South Coast AQMD (covering the Los Angeles basin and Orange County) and the Bay Area AQMD. These regulations directly affect which deck sealers and stains can be legally sold and applied in these districts.

For exterior wood stains and sealers, SCAQMD Rule 1113 currently limits VOC content to 100 g/L for semi-transparent stains and 50 g/L for clear sealers. Many of the premium oil-based sealers that top our performance rankings — including TWP 100, Ready Seal, and the standard Penofin formula — have VOC levels well above these limits (often 250–400 g/L) and cannot be legally applied in SCAQMD-regulated areas.

⚠️ California Compliance Warning If you live in the SCAQMD (Los Angeles, Orange, Riverside, and San Bernardino counties) or BAAQMD (Bay Area), you must use a compliant low-VOC formula. Applying non-compliant products is a violation of Rule 1113 and can result in fines. Retailers in these districts are legally prohibited from selling non-compliant products.

Compliant options for California homeowners include: DEFY Extreme (all formulas), Penofin Verde (specifically formulated as a low-VOC alternative to standard Penofin), and any product labeled "California Formula" or displaying a SCAQMD compliance marking. Performance of compliant formulas has improved significantly in the past several years — DEFY Extreme and Penofin Verde both delivered respectable test results in our evaluation, though neither matched the 24-month performance of TWP 100 or Armstrong Clark.

If you're outside of regulated districts (most of rural California, other Western states), standard oil-based formulas remain legal and continue to outperform water-based alternatives on raw penetration depth, color retention, and coverage interval.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you use regular deck stain on redwood?

Yes, you can use most general deck stains on redwood, but the results vary significantly depending on the formula. Redwood has several characteristics that make it a more demanding substrate than generic softwoods: higher natural oil content, elevated tannin levels, and a coarser, more open grain structure than pine or fir. These characteristics mean that latex-based (water-based) deck stains may not adhere as uniformly on oily redwood, particularly if the wood hasn't been properly cleaned with a wood brightener first. High-tannin redwood can also cause water-based stains to show a temporary discoloration (greenish or brown cast) on application as the tannins react with the formula's chemistry. Oil-based penetrating stains generally perform better because their chemistry is compatible with redwood's natural oils rather than being repelled by them. Before using any general deck stain on redwood, test a small hidden area first and confirm proper penetration and adhesion. Products specifically formulated for cedar and redwood (TWP 100, Penofin, Ready Seal) will generally outperform general-purpose deck stains on this specific substrate.

How do you stop redwood from graying?

The only effective way to prevent redwood from graying is to apply a UV-blocking sealer before UV degradation begins — ideally within 30–60 days of installation. Graying is caused by ultraviolet radiation breaking down lignin in the wood's surface fibers, and no amount of cleaning or maintenance can reverse this process once it's underway without a proper bleach-and-brightener treatment. The best sealers for preventing graying are pigmented semi-transparent oil-based formulas (TWP 100, Armstrong Clark, Penofin) because they use both organic pigments and UV absorbers to block the UV wavelengths responsible for lignin degradation. True clear sealers without UV-absorbing additives offer minimal protection against graying — they block moisture but not UV. If you prefer a clear finish, look specifically for products labeled "with UV protection" or "UV inhibitors." Maintenance is also critical: even the best sealer will eventually fade and allow UV exposure to resume. Following the manufacturer's recommended reapplication schedule is the only way to maintain ongoing UV protection. Our testing showed that TWP 100 on an annual maintenance schedule (clean and assess, reapply if needed) maintained near-original redwood color for the full two-year testing period.

Do you need to seal new redwood?

Yes — and ideally within the first month or two of installation, once the wood has dried and passed the soak test. While redwood's natural tannins and oils do provide genuine resistance to rot and insect damage, they provide essentially no protection against the two primary forms of surface degradation that affect new outdoor redwood: UV graying and surface checking from moisture cycling. Fresh, unstained redwood will begin to show UV-induced color fading within the first 3–6 months of sun exposure, and surface checking (small cracks along the grain) can develop within the first season of weather cycling. Beyond aesthetics, checking increases the wood's moisture absorption surface area and can eventually allow moisture to reach zones where structural decay begins. Sealing new redwood also locks in the tannins and prevents tannin bleed — the reddish-brown staining of adjacent masonry and concrete that is a common complaint from homeowners who delay sealing. One important caveat: very fresh redwood from the lumber yard may still have mill glaze or high moisture content that prevents sealer penetration. Perform the soak test (sprinkle water on the surface — if it beads up without absorbing, wait longer) before applying. For most lumber yard redwood, 2–4 weeks of drying time and a light cleaning is sufficient preparation.

How long does sealer last on redwood?

The honest answer is that coverage interval depends heavily on which product you use, the orientation and exposure of the surface, your climate, and maintenance practices. In our testing across two full seasons, coverage intervals ranged from approximately 1 year (Thompson's WaterSeal on a south-facing deck in Northern California) to 3+ years (Armstrong Clark on protected horizontal surfaces in moderate climates). Premium oil-based penetrating formulas in our test — TWP 100, Armstrong Clark, and Penofin — consistently outperformed water-based and budget options. As a general guideline: premium oil-based penetrating formulas last 2–4 years on protected vertical surfaces (fences, siding) and 1.5–2.5 years on horizontal deck surfaces that take direct foot traffic, sun, and moisture. Budget water-based products typically last 1–2 years on vertical surfaces and less than 1 year on high-traffic horizontal decks. Climate matters significantly: coastal California with salt air and high UV accelerates degradation; Oregon with high moisture and moderate UV presents different challenges. The practical test is simple: if water no longer beads up on the surface within 30 seconds, it's time to clean and reapply — don't wait for the finish to visibly fail.

What's the best finish for redwood furniture vs. decks?

Redwood furniture and redwood decks have different requirements that call for different product strategies. For decks — high-traffic horizontal surfaces subject to foot traffic, water pooling, UV, and weather — a penetrating oil-based semi-transparent stain/sealer (TWP 100, Armstrong Clark) is the best choice. These formulas penetrate the wood rather than forming a surface film, so they don't peel or chip under foot traffic the way film-forming products (like varnish or solid paint) would. They can also be reapplied without stripping. For redwood furniture — chairs, tables, planters, benches — that may be stored seasonally or used in a sheltered location, you have more options. A pure tung oil or linseed oil finish (Cabot Australian Timber Oil, Penofin) applied annually is a beautiful, natural-looking approach that provides good protection without altering the wood's tactile quality. For furniture that will be left outdoors year-round in harsh conditions, an exterior varnish or spar urethane topcoat over a penetrating oil base provides maximum durability, though it will require stripping when it eventually fails. For furniture in mild climates or sheltered applications, a quality semi-transparent penetrating oil applied seasonally is the most maintainable and aesthetically pleasing long-term approach.

Penofin vs. TWP — which is better for redwood?

Both Penofin and TWP 100 are excellent choices for redwood, and our testing found genuine performance differences that make each one better in specific scenarios. TWP 100 scored higher on UV color retention (12% fade at 24 months vs. 15% for Penofin), longer coverage interval (2–3 years vs. 1.5–2 years for Penofin in high-sun conditions), and overall color depth on smooth-planed redwood. Penofin scored better on penetration depth on rough-sawn surfaces (4–5mm end grain) and delivered a marginally more natural, less-dark finish appearance that some homeowners prefer. Penofin also offers a California VOC-compliant version (Penofin Verde) that TWP 100 does not — this is the decisive factor for California homeowners in regulated air districts. For a standard redwood deck in an unregulated state, TWP 100 is our top performer. For California-based projects requiring VOC compliance, Penofin Verde is the premium oil-based choice. For rough-sawn redwood fencing where deep penetration matters more than UV longevity, Penofin's ultra-penetrating formula gives it a narrow edge. Both products require similar preparation, have comparable application methods, and are in the same price range — the right choice comes down to your specific location, VOC requirements, and surface type.
