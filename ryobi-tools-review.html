Is Ryobi Worth Buying? Our Honest Assessment

Let's cut to the chase: Ryobi is not a professional-grade tool brand. If you're a carpenter running 10-hour days swinging a framing nailer five days a week, you should be looking at Milwaukee, Makita, or DeWalt. No amount of marketing copy will change that reality, and we're not here to convince you otherwise.

But here's what's equally true: for the vast majority of homeowners, weekend warriors, and serious DIYers, Ryobi's current lineup — especially the brushless ONE+ tools — represents some of the best value available in power tools today. After three years of running Ryobi tools alongside professional-grade alternatives in our working shop, we've developed a nuanced and sometimes surprising opinion of what the brand gets right and where it genuinely falls short.

The transformation Ryobi has undergone in the past four to five years is not trivial. The introduction of the brushless motor technology to the ONE+ lineup has genuinely closed the performance gap with premium brands on many tasks. The circular saw we tested runs cooler, faster, and with noticeably better cut quality than the brushed version it replaced. The drill driver develops torque figures that would have been unthinkable for a Ryobi tool five years ago. These are real improvements, not incremental tweaks.

At the same time, Ryobi's cost savings have to come from somewhere. Where we consistently notice them is in fit-and-finish, in the quality of accessory packages, and occasionally in long-term durability on tools that see daily abuse. The plastics feel slightly less robust than Milwaukee's. The depth stops and bevel adjustments on saws require more frequent calibration checks. The dust management on sanders isn't as refined as Festool or even Ridgid. These are real trade-offs you should know about before buying.

Our overall verdict after three years of daily use: Ryobi is genuinely excellent for homeowners and DIYers, genuinely adequate for occasional professional use as a backup set, and not the right primary choice for trades professionals. The price advantage is real — typically 30–50% cheaper than Milwaukee for equivalent tools — and when you're outfitting a home shop rather than a job-site truck, that difference matters enormously.

One thing that often gets overlooked in Ryobi discussions is the sheer breadth of the ONE+ ecosystem. With over 280 tools running on the same 18V battery platform, the system lock-in argument actually works in Ryobi's favor for homeowners. You'll never need to buy batteries for your leaf blower, your work light, your drill, and your jigsaw separately. That cross-compatibility has genuine practical value that's hard to put a dollar figure on but absolutely real when you're working a project.

We tested 10 Ryobi tools specifically for this review, choosing the most purchased and most searched tools in the lineup. We tested them against comparable tools from Milwaukee, DeWalt, and Makita where relevant. Our scores reflect real-world performance on actual projects — cutting framing lumber, sanding furniture, demolishing an old deck, installing flooring, and more.

Ryobi ONE+ Battery System Explained

The Ryobi ONE+ battery system is, frankly, one of the most compelling reasons to buy into the Ryobi ecosystem — and also one of the most important things to understand before you commit. The ONE+ platform uses 18V lithium-ion batteries and has maintained backward compatibility across the entire lineup since 1996. That's not a typo — nearly 30 years of compatibility. An old battery from a 2010 Ryobi drill will still power a 2026 brushless circular saw, and the reverse is also true.

This commitment to compatibility is strategically different from Milwaukee's M18 system, which also maintains long-term compatibility but at a significantly higher price point per battery. It's also different from DeWalt, which has maintained compatibility but occasionally fragmented the lineup with different platforms (20V MAX, FLEXVOLT, etc.) that don't always cross-charge cleanly.

Battery Sizes and What They Mean for You

Ryobi ONE+ batteries come in several capacities, measured in amp-hours (Ah). The most common sizes you'll encounter are 1.5Ah, 2.0Ah, 4.0Ah, 6.0Ah, and the newer high-capacity 12Ah packs. Here's a practical breakdown:

  • 1.5Ah and 2.0Ah: Good for light tools — the inflator, the work light, the radio. They're included in starter kits because they're cheap to manufacture, but you'll be recharging constantly on power-hungry tools.
  • 4.0Ah: The sweet spot for most homeowners. This is what we recommend pairing with drills, saws, and sanders. Enough runtime for most project sessions without excessive weight.
  • 6.0Ah: Better for high-demand tools like the circular saw or string trimmer. Good if you're running a tool for extended periods without a charger nearby.
  • 12Ah (PCK003): The flagship battery introduced in 2023. Exceptional runtime but heavy enough to affect tool balance on handheld tools. Best for stationary or semi-stationary use.

Charging Times

Standard Ryobi chargers are slow — the included PCL503 charger tops a 4.0Ah battery in about 60 minutes. The PCL541 rapid charger cuts that to around 30 minutes. If you're running tools all day, the rapid charger is worth the investment. We'd actually argue it should be included with any kit over $150, and we hope Ryobi eventually figures this out.

Is the Battery System Lock-In a Problem?

Battery platform lock-in is a real consideration in any cordless tool purchase. Once you own 4–6 Ryobi batteries, switching brands means either abandoning that investment or running two parallel battery ecosystems (which is annoying and expensive). Here's how we think about it for Ryobi specifically:

If you're buying your first cordless tool ever, the lock-in question is a feature, not a problem. You can commit to ONE+ knowing you're entering one of the largest and most affordable cordless ecosystems in the world. You'll never need to buy a new battery platform for any home task — Ryobi has ONE+ versions of table saws, chainsaws, pressure washers, wet/dry vacs, shop fans, and dozens of other tools.

If you're already invested in Milwaukee or DeWalt, Ryobi doesn't make sense as a replacement — but it might make sense as a supplemental outdoor/light-duty kit. The 40V outdoor tools use a separate battery platform (not ONE+), which is worth noting if you're considering mixing the 18V and 40V lineups.

The 40V Platform (Outdoor Tools)

Ryobi's outdoor power equipment uses a separate 40V battery system rather than the ONE+ 18V platform. This makes engineering sense — outdoor tools like string trimmers, leaf blowers, and push mowers need more sustained power than 18V can efficiently deliver — but it does mean you're buying into a second ecosystem if you want to combine indoor and outdoor Ryobi tools. The 40V batteries are not interchangeable with 18V ONE+ batteries.

The PCB575B is the single best argument for Ryobi's current relevance in the power tool market. BRAD NAILER
Ryobi ONE+ 18V Brad Nailer (PCL100B)
Best Nailer
★★★★★
4.5
(2,234 reviews)

Pneumatic nailers have long been the professional standard, and for very high-volume work — say, nailing off 50 squares of fascia — a compressor-and-hose setup is still faster and cheaper per nail. But for the homeowner tackling trim work, baseboard installation, crown molding, or cabinet face frames, the PCL100B brad nailer eliminates the compressor entirely and makes the entire process dramatically more convenient. We used it to install baseboards in a 1,400 square foot home — roughly 300 linear feet of material — and it performed admirably.

The 18-gauge capability covers the full range of standard brad nail lengths from 5/8" to 2-1/8". Tool-free depth adjustment lets you dial in countersink depth for different materials without stopping to grab a screwdriver. The sequential and bump-fire modes are both functional — sequential gives you more control and accuracy for precision work, while bump-fire speeds up repetitive nailing on long straight runs. We recommend sequential mode for trim work and bump-fire only for utility applications where aesthetics don't matter.

Drive consistency is the most important performance metric on a nailer, and the PCL100B is solid but not flawless. We experienced two misfires in approximately 800 nails driven — a 99.75% success rate that we'd consider acceptable for a battery-powered nailer but slightly below the 99.9%+ reliability you'd expect from a pneumatic tool. Jam clearing requires removing the magazine and is a bit fiddlier than on some competitors. The dry-fire lockout prevents you from driving nails into material with an empty magazine, which is a thoughtful feature that protects both the workpiece and the tool.

At roughly 5.5 lbs with battery, it's heavier than a pneumatic nailer (which has the compressor weight distributed elsewhere), and extended overhead nailing does cause arm fatigue. That said, eliminating the hose and compressor outweighs the weight penalty for most homeowner applications. The tool-free jam clearing and the no-mar tip (which protects finished surfaces from denting) are both practical design wins.

The PCL525B jigsaw is a versatile and capable tool for any homeowner who needs to cut curves, make plunge cuts, or work with irregular shapes. At 3,200 strokes per minute and with four orbital action settings, it offers enough speed and aggressiveness to cut through 3/4" plywood at a reasonable pace while also being controllable enough for precision curved cuts. We used it to cut sink cutouts in a kitchen countertop template, shape curved cabinet supports, and cut around electrical boxes during a basement finishing project.

The four-position orbital action is genuinely useful. Setting 0 (straight up-and-down stroke) is ideal for cutting metals, laminates, and fine finish cuts where blade deflection would cause problems. Settings 1 through 3 add progressive forward momentum to the blade stroke, which significantly increases cut speed in wood at the expense of some cut quality. For rough framing cuts, setting 3 is dramatically faster. Understanding how to use the orbital settings correctly is what separates frustrating jigsaw experiences from genuinely productive ones.

Tool-free blade change works cleanly and handles T-shank blades (the universal standard). The integrated dust blower keeps the cut line visible, which is important for following curved layout lines accurately. The LED work light illuminates the cut area well, particularly in the darker recesses of a cabinet or countertop installation. One issue we encountered: the base plate wobbles slightly at the pivot point when set to an angle, introducing variability on bevel cuts — this is a fit-and-finish issue that doesn't appear on Milwaukee's Jigsaw at twice the price.

The 4.3-star rating reflects a tool that handles the majority of homeowner jigsaw tasks well but has enough small imperfections to keep it from feeling truly premium. For cutting curves in wood and plywood — the most common DIY jigsaw use case — it's excellent. For precision metalwork or complex finish carpentry, the slight base wobble and occasional blade deflection will frustrate you.

Impact drivers have largely replaced standard drill-drivers for fastening tasks among experienced DIYers, and the PCF110B is a solid reason why. Developing 1,700 inch-pounds of torque at up to 3,200 impacts per minute, it drives large fasteners — deck screws, structural screws, lag bolts — with significantly less effort and wrist strain than a drill-driver. We used it for a deck framing project involving several hundred 3" structural screws and came away impressed with both the power and the battery efficiency.

The three-speed setting system is one of the PCF110B's most practical features. Speed 2 (medium, 0–2,400 IPM) handles most general fastening tasks. Speed 3 (high, 0–3,200 IPM) is for large fasteners in demanding applications. This graduated approach gives you much better control than a single-speed impact driver, and we appreciate that Ryobi included it rather than reserving it for a more expensive model.

The compact head length — shorter than the drill-driver — makes it easier to use in tight spaces, particularly around cabinet interiors and stair stringers. The magnetic bit holder is strong enough to retain bits reliably even during the impact pulses, which isn't true of all impact drivers. The LED work light illuminates the fastening point clearly at close range.

Where the PCF110B loses ground is in the 1/4" hex chuck — shared by virtually all impact drivers in this class, so not a Ryobi-specific issue, but worth noting. You'll need a special adapter for socket drive work. The variable speed trigger is good but not as finely controllable as Milwaukee's FUEL impact driver at low speeds. Still, at this price point, the three-speed setting system compensates well for the trigger sensitivity limitation.

The TSS103 is a corded tool — 15 amps, 4,500 RPM — and represents Ryobi's approach to stationary shop tools: reasonable power, solid feature set, and a price point that makes it accessible to homeowners who wouldn't otherwise own a sliding compound miter saw. We set this up in our shop for six months as our primary miter saw for trim work, molding cuts, and lumber dimensioning. It's a capable tool with some real limitations that buyers should understand before purchasing.

The 10" blade and sliding rail system can crosscut boards up to 12" wide — adequate for most trim and molding applications. The dual bevel (beveling both left and right without flipping the workpiece) is a genuine quality-of-life feature for cutting crown molding, and Ryobi's inclusion of it at this price point is commendable. The miter detents at common angles (0°, 15°, 22.5°, 31.6°, 45°) are positive and repeatable. The bevel detents at 0° and 45° are similarly crisp.

The sliding rails are the TSS103's biggest limitation. On the Milwaukee M18 FUEL cordless miter saw and on professional-grade corded Bosch and Makita saws, the rails are tight and virtually free of play. On the TSS103, we measured 1/32" of wobble in the rail system under normal cutting pressure — small in absolute terms but enough to cause visible inconsistency in miter cuts on wider boards. For 90-degree crosscuts and standard miter angles on trim, this is not a significant problem. For precision angles on hardwood joinery, it matters more.

The laser guide is useful for setup but requires occasional recalibration. The dust bag captures about 50% of dust — worse than the sander's dust system, and miter saws generate enormous quantities of fine dust. We connect it to a shop vacuum via the included port during most operations. For a homeowner's shop saw that gets used on weekend projects rather than all day every day, the TSS103 is good value. For a professional shop, it would be a frustrating primary tool.

INFLATOR

The PCL706B inflator is, on paper, the least exciting tool on this list. Frequently Asked Questions

Are Ryobi brushless tools worth the extra cost over brushed?

Yes, in almost every case. The price difference between brushless and brushed Ryobi tools has narrowed significantly as the brushless lineup has matured. In most tool categories, brushless models are now $20–$40 more than their brushed counterparts — a difference that is easily justified by the performance gains.

Brushless motors provide two primary advantages: efficiency and longevity. Efficiency means longer runtime per battery charge — typically 20–30% more tasks completed per charge cycle in our testing. Longevity means the motor doesn't have brushes that physically wear down against the commutator; the motor's lifespan is substantially longer. For tools you'll use frequently, brushless is the right buy. For tools you'll use rarely (the inflator, a work light), the brushed equivalent might make more sense.

Additionally, the brushless circular saw and drill driver perform at levels that genuinely compete with mid-tier professional tools — something the brushed versions could not claim. If you're buying new Ryobi tools in 2026, we recommend defaulting to brushless whenever the price difference is less than $50.

How many Ryobi batteries do I need to start?

For most homeowners, two 4.0Ah batteries and one rapid charger is the minimum comfortable starting configuration. Here's the reasoning: most projects require more than one battery session — when battery #1 is depleted, battery #2 goes in while #1 charges. With the rapid charger and two batteries in rotation, you can work indefinitely on a project without stopping to wait for a charge.

Starting with only one battery forces you to stop for 45–60 minutes every time it depletes, which is genuinely frustrating on active project days. The cost of a second battery (around $50–$60 for a 4.0Ah) is well worth it.

If you're running multiple tools simultaneously — like a drill and a circular saw — three batteries gives you even more flexibility. In our shop, we keep four 4.0Ah batteries in rotation for days when multiple people are working simultaneously. For a single homeowner doing weekend projects, two is sufficient and three is comfortable.

Can Ryobi tools handle professional-quality work?

The honest answer is: it depends on what you mean by "professional-quality work." If the question is whether Ryobi tools can produce professional-quality results — precise cuts, properly driven fasteners, smooth sanded surfaces — then yes, they absolutely can in the hands of a skilled operator. The tool's limitations rarely determine output quality as much as the operator's skill and technique.

If the question is whether Ryobi tools can withstand professional-level daily abuse and use volumes — 8–10 hours of daily use, 5 days a week, year after year — then no, they're not the right choice. Motor durability, housing integrity, and calibration stability all degrade faster under heavy professional use compared to Milwaukee or DeWalt professional-grade tools.

For occasional professional use — a contractor who needs a backup drill or a circular saw for situations where the primary kit is out for service — Ryobi is adequate. For primary professional use in a trades business, we recommend investing in Milwaukee or Makita.

Are old Ryobi ONE+ batteries compatible with new tools?

Yes, with one important caveat. The ONE+ battery form factor has been consistent since 1996, and all 18V ONE+ batteries will physically fit and power all ONE+ tools. However, some older batteries — particularly original NiCd packs from the early 2000s — have significantly lower performance than current lithium-ion packs and will severely limit modern brushless tools. Brushless motors expect a consistent voltage delivery that older NiCd chemistry cannot provide under load.

For practical purposes: any lithium-ion ONE+ battery from 2010 or newer will work with modern ONE+ tools. NiCd batteries should be retired and properly recycled (most hardware stores accept them for recycling). Additionally, some very high-current tools (like the ONE+ chainsaw) include documentation specifying minimum battery size requirements — check the manual for your specific tool.

The reverse is also true: new high-capacity batteries (6Ah, 12Ah) will work in older tools, but those tools won't take advantage of the extra capacity — they'll simply run longer before the battery depletes.

Should I buy Ryobi or wait and save up for Milwaukee?

This depends entirely on your use case, which brings us back to the central theme of this review. If you're a homeowner who will use these tools for weekend projects and home maintenance — which describes the vast majority of people buying power tools — Ryobi is the right answer now. You don't need Milwaukee performance for occasional home use, and the money you save buys more materials, more tools, or more project enjoyment.

If you're entering a trade professionally and plan to use these tools to earn income, Milwaukee or Makita is the right long-term investment. The higher cost per tool is offset by the longer lifespan, better warranty service, and the professional reputation that comes with using recognized professional-grade tools on job sites.

The "save up for Milwaukee" argument often assumes that Milwaukee is simply a better version of the same thing — it's not. Milwaukee is a different category of tool for a different user profile. Buying Milwaukee as a homeowner is often like buying a heavy-duty pickup truck when you need a sensible sedan. You'll have more capability than you need, you'll pay more for it, and you'll spend more on maintenance. Ryobi's current brushless lineup is genuinely excellent for homeowner use in 2026, and there's no reason to delay your projects waiting for a tool upgrade you don't need.
