Before You Buy

5 Things to Know Before Buying a Sheet Metal Brake

Avoid the most common buying mistakes — we've seen them all.

📏 Width vs. Working Width

A "48-inch brake" describes the full jaw width — but usable working width is typically 2–4 inches less. Always check the actual bending capacity, not the model name.

🔩 Gauge Ratings Are Optimistic

Manufacturers rate capacity for mild steel at 90°. Bending aluminum at the same gauge is easier; bending stainless or going past 120° will exceed rated capacity quickly.

📦 Cornice vs. Box & Pan

Cornice brakes bend the full width in one pass — fast for flashing and trim. Box & pan brakes have removable fingers for multi-sided forms. Know which geometry you need before buying.

⚖️ Weight = Rigidity

Heavier beds flex less. A 100-lb hobby brake will crown on 18-gauge over 36 inches. If you need consistent bend angles across full-width bends, buy heavier than you think you need.

🔧 Parts & Service Matter

Blades, fingers, and wear parts need replacement. Check that the manufacturer has US stock, not just slow-ship from overseas. Tennsmith and Grizzly win on parts availability.

🏭 Match It to Your Volume

A $300 bench brake is fine for 50 bends a month. At 500 bends a month, you'll wear it out in a year. Match the tool's duty cycle rating to your actual production pace.
